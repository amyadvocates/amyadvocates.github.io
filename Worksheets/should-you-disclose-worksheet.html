"Should You Disclose?"

A structured guide to help you decide whether, when, and how to discuss neurodivergence or disability at work.

This worksheet follows the 30-minute workshop. Work through each page at your own pace — pause, reflect, and return at any time.

There are no right or wrong answers. The aim is to make the most informed decision available to you now.

By the end of this worksheet, you will have:

1
Assessed your manager and organisation
2
Considered the benefits and risks of disclosure
3
Chosen a primary and backup route
4
Decided what to share and what to ask for
5
Identified one concrete next step

You do not need a formal diagnosis, a perfect explanation of how your brain works, or a complete list of workplace adjustments to take part. You simply need to be considering whether something about your health, neurodivergence, or way of working needs to be discussed at work.

Have your notes ready — or use the text boxes on each page. Use the Next button below to move through the worksheet one section at a time.

Setting the Scene

Disclosure is a spectrum — not a single yes-or-no.

You don't have to decide everything at once, or share everything at once.

How much personal information might I share?

Share nothing personal yet Share a diagnosis
Describe the work impact Mention a condition

Gathering information is an action you can take at any stage — not a disclosure level. Reading your employer's policy, speaking to a union representative, or asking HR how the process works are all things you can do before, during or instead of sharing any personal information.

It also helps to separate two decisions:

What am I willing to share?

The personal information you're comfortable disclosing to this person, at this point.

What do I want them to do?

The action or response you're looking for from the other person.

Where are you starting?

Choose the option that comes closest to where you are right now. There is no correct place to be.

You might want to understand your options, identify the right person to approach, get a practical change, or reduce some of the uncertainty. You don't need to solve the whole situation today.

Part One: Assess Your Workplace Context

What has your manager actually shown you?

Assess behaviour — not personality. Look for evidence, not impressions.

A manager can be warm and friendly while being unreliable with sensitive issues. A direct, task-focused manager may be consistent, fair, and willing to make practical changes.

For each question, choose: Yes / Mixed / No / Not enough evidence. Don't force an answer when you haven't seen enough — "not enough evidence" is different from having seen mixed behaviour.

Question 1 of 6

Does my manager listen before reaching a conclusion?

When you explain a difficulty, do they ask questions and try to understand — or do they interrupt, minimise it, or immediately tell you what they think the problem is?

Question 1: Does my manager listen before reaching a conclusion?

Question 2 of 6

Can they respond to a concern without becoming defensive?

What happens when you say an instruction was unclear, a workload is unrealistic, or something isn't working? Do they treat it as useful information — or hear it as criticism?

Question 2: Can they respond to a concern without becoming defensive?

Question 3 of 6

Do they follow through?

They may respond warmly in the moment — but do they remember what was agreed? Do they make the change? Do they check in again? Supportive words without follow-through may leave you doing all the work of raising the issue repeatedly.

Question 3: Do they follow through?

Question 4 of 6

Do they handle sensitive information carefully?

Have you seen them gossip about employees, speculate about someone's health, or share information that didn't need to be shared? Or do they demonstrate discretion? You can only assess the evidence available to you — not guarantee confidentiality.

Question 4: Do they handle sensitive information carefully?

Question 5 of 6

Can they adapt how they manage different people?

Do they accept that one person may need written instructions, another more notice, another regular check-ins — or do they see treating everyone identically as fairness? A manager doesn't need to understand every form of neurodivergence, but they do need some willingness to adapt.

Question 5: Can they adapt how they manage different people?

Question 6 of 6

Can they say, "I don't know, but I'll find out"?

A manager doesn't need to be a neurodiversity expert. A manager who confidently assumes they understand your diagnosis may sometimes be harder to work with than someone who acknowledges the limits of their knowledge and seeks advice.

Question 6: Can they say "I don't know, but I'll find out"?

Part One: Assess Your Workplace Context

What does the evidence tell you?

Don't just total the answers. Look for the weight of the evidence — and pay attention to any serious red flags.

Try to use a specific example, not a general impression. For example: "When I raised an unrealistic deadline, she listened and helped me reprioritise."

For example: "He agreed to confirm instructions in writing but stopped doing it after one week."

Note on red flags: One serious concern can outweigh several positive answers. A manager who repeatedly dismisses concerns, breaks confidence, retaliates, changes expectations without warning, or fails to follow agreed actions gives you meaningful evidence to take seriously — even if other answers were mostly positive.

Assess the organisation

Your manager is only one part of the system. Assess two things separately: how mature the process is, and what the culture is actually like.

Rate process clarity from 1 to 5

Culture safety — how safe does the culture appear?

1 = people are judged or sidelined when they need support  |  5 = people can discuss disability and access needs without it affecting how their capability is viewed

What do your scores suggest?

A large organisation can have impressive policy but a difficult culture. A small organisation may have no formal programme but still respond flexibly. This is why we assess them separately.

Part Two: Understand What Disclosure May Change

Legal context and practical principles

A brief overview of what telling an employer can change — not individual legal advice.

General information only. Employment and disability law varies between countries and depends on your employer's size, your employment status, and the facts of your situation. Seek appropriate local advice if you face dismissal, disciplinary action, serious discrimination, retaliation or an urgent risk to your health or safety.

Three practical principles

1. You don't usually need to share your whole medical history.

There is a difference between explaining the work-related effects of a condition and sharing your entire personal history. You can describe the impact without sharing everything.

"I have a condition that affects working memory and processing speed. I would like important instructions and changes to priorities confirmed in writing."

2. Your employer generally needs enough information to recognise there may be a disability-related need.

There's a meaningful difference between "I prefer written instructions" and "Because of a condition that affects how I process verbal information, I need important instructions confirmed in writing." The amount you share can affect how the request is interpreted and handled.

3. Legal protection does not guarantee an emotionally safe or competent response from every manager.

The law is one part of your decision. The evidence you have about your workplace is another.

Part Three: Weigh Up the Decision

Benefits, concerns, and urgency

Think about whether starting a particular conversation, with a particular person, at this point, is likely to improve your situation.

What might improve if I start a conversation?

Choose the two benefits that matter most to you.

What am I concerned could happen?

Try not to dismiss your concerns as negativity — some are based on uncertainty, others on genuine evidence. Choose the two that feel most important.

How I may be perceived

Capability · judgement · personality

How the information may be handled

Confidentiality · requests for evidence · discrimination or retaliation

Whether the conversation will help

No practical action · oversharing · unclear support · performance or formal processes

A concern can still matter even if it has not happened before, particularly when the possible consequence is serious.

How urgent is my need?

Select your urgency level

Higher risk does not always mean "do not disclose." It may mean: do not disclose alone, informally, or without preparation. It could mean speaking to a union representative, putting the request in writing, or seeking advice about what to disclose and how to document it.

Part Four: Choose Your Route and Plan Your First Step

Who should you approach? What are you willing to share? What do you want them to do?

Choose the route most likely to give you useful movement with an acceptable level of risk — and pick a backup.

The five routes

Route 1: Your manager
Best when there is reasonable trust, they control the relevant parts of your work, and they've shown some ability to listen and act. Most useful for small practical changes around meetings, workload, communication, or deadlines.
Route 2: HR or People team
May be better when you need to understand policy, create a formal record, or avoid your manager being the sole gatekeeper. HR represents the organisation. Before sharing details, you can ask: "How would this information be recorded, who would have access, and what are the next steps?"
Route 3: Occupational Health (or equivalent)
Useful when work is affecting you but you can't yet identify the right changes. The availability and independence of OH varies — find out what it can and cannot do before assuming it is confidential or decisive.
Route 4: Union or employee representative
Can help you understand your rights, prepare for a meeting, review written communication, or accompany you. Particularly important if a performance, capability, grievance, or disciplinary process has already begun.
Route 5: Employee network or trusted internal contact
Can provide useful information about how processes work in practice. But a network is not automatically a confidential legal or clinical service — ask about its boundaries before sharing identifiable information.

Your backup matters because your first contact may not be able to resolve the issue.

Part Five: Decide What Happens Next

Your first step

A good next step gives you more information, support, or control. It doesn't have to be "disclose to my manager."

You have now considered: what your manager has shown you; how mature the organisation appears; the possible benefits and risks; the urgency of your need; and the primary and backup routes you might use, what you are willing to share, and what you want the other person to do.

The final task is to turn that thinking into one manageable action.

Some possible next steps

  • Read the disability or accommodations policy.
  • Ask HR to explain the process without yet sharing personal information.
  • Contact a union representative.
  • Ask whether Occupational Health is available.
  • Speak to someone in an employee network about their experience.
  • Write a draft email and leave it overnight.
  • Book a meeting with your manager.
  • Ask a trusted person to review what you plan to say.
  • Decide you're not ready, and set a specific date to reconsider.

Make it specific enough that you'll know when you've done it. Instead of "Look into support" — write "Search the intranet for the adjustments or accommodations policy on Monday morning." Instead of "Talk to my manager" — write "Draft a three-sentence email asking for a private meeting."

Three things to keep in mind before any conversation

🎯
Purpose

Be clear about what you're asking for and why — before you begin.

🗺️
Route

Choose a route that reflects the evidence you have about your workplace.

🛡️
Boundary

Know what you are — and are not — ready to share before you start.

"I'm not asking you to be an expert. I'd like to explain what I've noticed about how this is affecting me at work and agree an appropriate next step."

After any substantive conversation: send a short written summary of what was discussed, what was agreed, who will take the next action, and when it will be reviewed.

Think of the process as: Prepare → Ask → Confirm in writing → Review.

The aim is not to make the bravest decision. It is to make the most informed decision available to you now.
